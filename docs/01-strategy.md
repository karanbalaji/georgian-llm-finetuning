# Part I — Strategy & Vision: Scaling Transferred Learnings

## 12-Month Vision

**Season 1 of Transferred Learnings: from a 1,000-member mailing list to the default technical home for applied AI builders in Canada — with a recognized presence in SF and NYC.**

Today TL has scale (1,000+ members) but not gravity — it's lightly active and not differentiated from any other AI newsletter or Slack. The job in Year 1 isn't to grow the number; it's to earn a reputation: the place where practitioners come for real technical depth, not vendor content, and where Georgian's access to applied research, technical talent, and portfolio companies becomes visible *through the community itself* rather than through marketing copy.

Talent, partnerships, and investment insight are the second-order outcomes of that reputation — not the pitch. If TL is genuinely useful to builders first, Georgian becomes a natural hub for talent and deal flow as a byproduct.

## Core Audiences, in Priority Order

1. **AI/LLM builders & engineers** — the base. If this group doesn't find real value, nothing else works. Primary geographic focus: Canada, with a deliberate SF/NYC push.
2. **Founders & technical leads** — Georgian portfolio companies first, broader ecosystem second. They come for the same technical substance as builders, but the relationship compounds into partnerships and pipeline.
3. **Researchers & academics** — via partners like the Vector Institute; they lend credibility and are a recruiting source for the Lab Series.
4. **Georgian internal stakeholders** (AI Lab, Investment, RevOps, portfolio) — beneficiaries of the community's momentum, not the audience being programmed for. If content is built to please internal stakeholders first, builders will feel it and disengage.

## The Content Engine: Three Series, One Season

Rather than a scattershot events calendar, TL's programming runs as three named, recurring series — each owned by one of the case study's three pillars, so every piece of content has a clear purpose and a clear owner:

| Series | Pillar | What it is |
|---|---|---|
| **Builder Series** | Talent Engagement & Access | Hands-on build sessions, live coding, and challenges. Highest talent signal — Georgian sees real working output, not attendance. |
| **Lab Series** | Research & Thought Leadership | Georgian AI Lab researchers presenting findings, open-source/toolkit deep-dives (starting with the LLM fine-tuning toolkit). |
| **Founder Talks** | Ecosystem Positioning & Relations | Founder and portfolio-company spotlights, conversations with partners like the Vector Institute. |

**Season 1 = Year 1.** Not quarters — a season is the whole year, matching the standard convention (a TV season, a cohort year), so the framing stays simple and the presentation itself can literally say "everything in this plan is Season 1." Flagship programs (like the Render Fine-Tune & Ship Challenge, see Part II) are tentpole episodes *within* Season 1, not season boundaries. Season 2 begins Year 2's vision, once Season 1's cadence is proven — deliberately not over-formalized (episode branding, numbering, visual identity) until then.

Every episode is distributed as a discrete unit through **Luma**, where TL already has a presence: city-based discovery, calendar subscriptions, and automatic subscriber growth per city come for free, and Luma's RSVP/attendance data doubles as a built-in metrics source — no new infrastructure needed.

## Sequencing & Sharp Prioritization

The brief asks for sharp prioritization, so here is the explicit order of operations for the first 90 days — and what Year 1 deliberately leaves out.

**First 90 days, in order:**
1. **Baseline before building** — audit TL's current member data and Luma history: who's actually active, in which cities, around what topics. Every later decision (ambassador cities, series topics) keys off this.
2. **Stand up Lab Series first** — it's the fastest credible win because the content already exists (AI Lab research, the fine-tuning toolkit); it re-activates the dormant list with substance before asking anyone to commit effort.
3. **Launch the Builder Series flagship** — the Render "Fine-Tune & Ship" Challenge (Part II) as the first tentpole: the moment TL shifts from content-you-consume to community-you-build-in.
4. **Recruit the first 3–5 ambassadors** — from the members those two programs surface. Ambassadors are recruited from demonstrated engagement, not applications from strangers, which is why this step comes last.

**What Year 1 deliberately won't do:**
- **No paid member acquisition** — growth must come from content quality and ambassador reach; paid growth would inflate exactly the vanity metric we've said doesn't matter.
- **No new platforms or custom infrastructure** — Luma + existing channels are enough; engineering effort goes into content and programs, not tooling.
- **No multi-city expansion before Toronto's cadence is proven** — SF/NYC enter only through ambassadors, not simultaneous owned events.
- **No gating content behind Georgian sales motions** — the moment TL feels like top-of-funnel for the fund, builders leave. Second-order outcomes stay second-order.

## The Community as a Trend-Sensing Engine

The brief's Research pillar asks Georgian to "track emerging AI trends through hands-on work and community insight" — and the community itself is the instrument. Ambassador field reports, office-hours questions, and challenge submissions are structured, recurring signal about what builders are actually adopting, struggling with, and abandoning — months before it shows up in market reports. Operationally: a **quarterly community-signal brief** delivered to Investment and the AI Lab, synthesizing what the community surfaced that quarter. This is the concrete mechanism by which "investment insights" become a real second-order outcome rather than a slide-deck aspiration.

## Transferred Learnings Ambassadors

The mechanism that lets TL scale into SF/NYC without headcount, and converts the most engaged builders into a standing pipeline. Modeled on four real precedents, researched directly rather than assumed:

- **[Stripe Community Builders](https://stripe.com/en-ca/guides/become-a-stripe-community-builder)** — city/industry-focused leaders who commit to running 4+ events/year and moderating an online community. In exchange: event-cost funding, access to Stripe experts as speakers, use of Stripe offices, and public recognition.
- **[Cerebras Ambassadors](https://www.cerebras.ai/ambassadors)** — AI builders who host local "Cafe Compute" meetups. In exchange: compute credits, venue/food funding, a global builder network (SF, London, Singapore, Tokyo, Mumbai), and a real feedback loop into Cerebras leadership.
- **[NVIDIA Developer Champions](https://developer.nvidia.com/developer-champions-program)** — selected for demonstrated technical proficiency and a public track record (GitHub, talks, hackathons), not credentials. In exchange: expert access, channel amplification, a public Champion Directory, merch, and event priority.
- **[OpenAI Codex Ambassadors](https://developers.openai.com/community/codex-ambassadors)** — proven community operators (meetup organizers, OSS maintainers) committing ~2–4 hrs/week. In exchange: API/product credits, pre-built starter kits, direct team access, and honorariums.

**Transferred Learnings Ambassadors, synthesized from that pattern:**
- **What ambassadors get:** a capped per-event cost stipend; Georgian AI Lab researchers/engineers available as speakers; Georgian office space where available (Toronto first); compute/API credits tied to the fine-tuning toolkit for their own demos; a "Builder Series in a box" starter kit they can localize instead of building content from scratch; public recognition (Ambassador badge, featured on TL channels, a public directory page).
- **What they commit to:** hosting at least 4 TL-branded events/year (Stripe's bar), reporting back what their local builders are working on and stuck on (Cerebras's feedback loop), and staying within a lightweight code of conduct.
- **Who gets selected:** proven community operators or technically credible builders (NVIDIA/OpenAI's bar) — not just enthusiastic members. Selectivity is what makes the recognition worth something.
- **Why it matters strategically:** ambassador-led events in SF/NYC solve the "leading technical hubs" requirement in the case study brief without opening an office there. And ambassadors are disproportionately likely to become the founders, hires, or portfolio-relevant relationships the other two pillars are optimizing for — the program is a talent and ecosystem pipeline disguised as an events program.

## Resourcing & Cross-Functional Collaboration

The **Lead AI Advocate** orchestrates; no single function can run this alone:
- **AI Lab** supplies the technical substance — research, the fine-tuning toolkit, speakers for Lab Series and Builder Series episodes.
- **Marketing & Events** handle production and promotion — Luma page management, recap content, cross-channel distribution.
- **RevOps & Investment** feed in portfolio-company intros and define what "quality" talent/deal signal actually looks like to them, so the community isn't optimizing for vanity metrics.
- **Portfolio companies** (like Render in Part II) co-host flagship episodes, supplying both technical credibility and a talent destination.
- **Partners** (e.g., Vector Institute) supply speakers and academic credibility for Founder Talks.

**Cadence:** one flagship, cross-functionally resourced program per quarter (the Render Challenge is the Q1 example), with lighter always-on Lab Series / Founder Talks content filling the gaps between. This avoids burning the whole team's bandwidth and budget on a single push, and gives the year a rhythm the panel can picture.

## Year-1 Success Metrics

Quality over vanity, deliberately:

- **Active contributor rate** (people who show up, build, or speak) over raw member count
- **Content reach/engagement** on Lab Series output — the direct proxy for "is Georgian AI Lab's thought leadership actually landing"
- **Community → portfolio-company talent conversions** — interviews or hires sourced from TL, the clearest talent-pipeline signal
- **Partnerships or deal flow** traceably connected to a community relationship
- **Event NPS**, tracked per series so underperforming formats can be cut early
- **Ambassador program health** — number of active ambassadors, ambassador-led events, and geographic spread outside Toronto (the direct measure of the SF/NYC push)
- **Luma subscriber growth per city** — the built-in, no-extra-infrastructure read on reach
- **Quarterly community-signal brief delivered to Investment/AI Lab** — shipped on time, and at least one insight per quarter that Investment flags as genuinely useful (the trend-sensing engine, measured)
