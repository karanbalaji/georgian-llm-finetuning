# Presentation Deck Outline

40-minute walkthrough + 20-minute Q&A, cross-functional panel (AI Lab, Marketing, Events, Investment, Community). Roughly 2–3 min/slide, leaving room for the live prototype walkthrough to breathe.

**This outline is built** — `/pitch`, 15 slides (14 main + an appendix). Presented live, in this app, at `/pitch?slide=1` (arrow keys / space to advance) — no separate export step: it's a real Next.js route reusing this app's own design system, with `motion`-driven slide and content-reveal animation.

**Audit note:** a review against `case-study.md` (the actual brief) found the deck was under-answering two of Part I's four explicit questions — "12-month vision **and its core audiences**" and "what initiatives would you **prioritize**." `docs/01-strategy.md` already had strong, specific answers to both (the Core Audiences section, and the "Sequencing & Sharp Prioritization" section with the 90-day order and the explicit "what Year 1 won't do" list) — they just hadn't made it from the strategy doc into the slides. Fixed two ways:

1. A **roadmap slide** (#2) right after the title, quoting all four Part I questions from the brief verbatim, so the panel sees immediately that the deck is structured to answer exactly what they asked, in order.
2. A dedicated **Core Audiences slide** (#4) and an interactive **quarterly vision timeline** (#6, see below) — rather than folding the content into existing slides, since the brief singles out "sharp prioritization" by name as something they're scoring for.

Most slides pair real content (left) with a supporting visual (right) — a resourcing wheel, a pillar→series mapping, a fine-tune→deploy pipeline, a real-data budget chart, a flywheel, etc. These are hand-built React/SVG components (`src/components/pitch/visuals.tsx`) with real text labels drawn from the actual content, not AI-generated images — a first pass using generated images looked generic and carried no real information (unlabeled shapes), so they were replaced with code that renders the actual pillar names, org roles, and dollar amounts already in the slide copy. Slides where the real content is already data-bearing (the ambassador citations, the Year-1 metrics checklist, the appendix risks table) skip a visual entirely and widen instead — a decorative shape next to real citation links would dilute them, not support them. See `src/app/pitch/slides.tsx` for the content, `src/components/pitch/primitives.tsx` for the slide shell/typography, and `src/components/pitch/visuals.tsx` / `VisionTimeline.tsx` for the diagrams.

1. **Title / who I am** — framed around a hybrid positioning: genuinely technical *and* a community/relationship builder, not one or the other.
2. **Roadmap** *(new)* — the brief's four Part I questions, quoted directly: vision & audiences, prioritization, resourcing, success metrics. Sets up that every slide from here answers one of these, in order.
3. **Current state of TL** — the problem statement: 1,000+ members, lightly active, not differentiated. Establish the stakes before pitching the fix. Bridges into the 12-month vision.
4. **Core audiences, in priority order** *(new)* — directly answers "…and its core audiences": builders & engineers first (the base), then founders/technical leads, researchers/academics, and Georgian internal stakeholders last — deliberately, since programming for internal stakeholders first is exactly what would make builders disengage.
5. **Three-pillar framework** — Research & Thought Leadership / Talent Engagement & Access / Ecosystem Positioning, each mapped to a named series (Lab / Builder / Founder Talks).
6. **The next 12 months, one quarter at a time** *(new, interactive)* — directly answers "what initiatives would you prioritize," in the brief's own language ("Sharp Prioritization" is the slide's eyebrow). A click-through quarterly timeline (`VisionTimeline.tsx`) — click Q1–Q4 to swap the detail panel — where Q1 *is* the explicit 90-day order of operations (baseline → Lab Series → Builder Series flagship → ambassadors) and Q2–Q4 extrapolate the stated "one flagship per quarter" cadence forward (second flagship + SF/NYC push, then proving Year 1 and drafting Season 2). Paired with the equally explicit "what Year 1 deliberately won't do" list — showing the cuts, not just the plan.
7. **Ambassador program** — Transferred Learnings Ambassadors, with the Stripe/Cerebras/NVIDIA/OpenAI precedent citations as evidence this is a proven mechanism, not a guess. Answers "how do you scale into SF/NYC without headcount."
8. **Community Partnerships** *(new)* — the fastest lever, and a deliberate complement to Ambassadors rather than a duplicate of it: Ambassadors recruit an individual and wait months for them to build local presence from near-zero; Community Partnerships co-host with Toronto AI/builder communities that already have an engaged audience and already run events — real, currently active examples cited directly ([AI Tinkerers Toronto](https://toronto.aitinkerers.org/), [BUILD FUTURE](https://buildfuture.ai/), the latter already proven to run events at other companies' offices). The mechanism is concrete, not aspirational: Georgian's Toronto office as the venue offer, and co-hosting the event on **Luma** specifically (submitting one event to another calendar so it appears on both, reaching the partner's existing follower base) — not just a cross-promotion post. Runs in parallel with the 90-day sequence from day one, since it needs no baseline audit or vetting cycle.
9. **Resourcing & cross-functional model** — who does what (Lead AI Advocate, AI Lab, Marketing/Events, RevOps/Investment, portfolio, partners), and the quarterly-flagship-plus-steady-content cadence.
10. **Year-1 success metrics** — grouped by the brief's own Objective language ("talent magnet," "trusted AI thought leader," "second-order outcomes") rather than an arbitrary column split, including a new metric for Community Partnerships: co-hosted events and net-new TL subscribers per event.
11. **Render "Fine-Tune & Ship" Challenge** — the Part II transition folded in ("here's Season 1's first Builder Series flagship, in practice"), why Render (the differentiation argument), and the flagship track — the local-first router, citing the Stanford finding that 71.3% of real ChatGPT queries are answerable by a local model.
12. **Timeline & budget** — the 8-week schedule (Launch → Build → Judge → Ship, matching the title's own four verbs row-for-row) and the $10K breakdown, both real numbers from the actual plan, not rounded placeholders.
13. **Proof** — the demo moment. Not a mockup of the fine-tune → export → deploy loop — it was actually run (a real LoRA fine-tune of Hermes-3 on a support-triage dataset, `finetune/results/before_after.md` shows genuine before/after output, and the resulting GGUF is a real, publicly downloadable Hugging Face model the panel can pull themselves).
14. **Why this hits both goals + the ask** — toolkit gets real usage and case-study content, Render gets to see actual working code from candidates before ever seeing a resume; program success metrics; one clear ask to close.
15. **Appendix: risks & mitigations** — Q&A prep (low signups, drop-off, submission quality, judging load), not part of the main 40-minute walkthrough.
